Sentry sees the error. LangSmith sees the trace. Neither sees these five things.

Intent

What people actually want from your server

The agent's goal, in plain language, before the call. This is your demand signal: the top things customers try to do, ranked by frequency, not by what your tools happen to be named.

Tool call

What gets used, and how

Which tools, which parameters, which retry patterns. Real usage shape, including the calls that get hammered and the ones nobody touches.

Expected

What the agent thought would happen

Captured before the call, not reconstructed after. This is the reference point everything else is measured against.

Observed

What actually happened

Error class, response body, duration. The gap between expected and observed is your silent failure rate: the interactions that returned 200 and still went nowhere.

Config

Why it works for some customers and not others

Permissions, settings, integration state at the moment of the call. The difference between the accounts your server delights and the ones it quietly breaks for.

The roadmap your competitors can't see

The most valuable thing your MCP server can tell you is what it can't do.

When an agent wants something your tools can't express, that request normally vanishes: no error, no log, no ticket. Baton captures it. Every "the agent tried to do X and there was no tool for it" is a roadmap item your competitors can't see, written by the customer who needed it.

You define the signal. You own the data.

  • You define the schema. You choose exactly which fields are captured and transmitted. What you don't ask for, nothing sends.
  • Emit anywhere. Ship to the Baton Console, any OpenTelemetry sink, or your own S3. The data is yours and it can stay inside your perimeter.
  • PII scrubbed at the source. Redaction happens in the SDK, before anything leaves the customer's runtime.
  • Scoped to what you instrument. Baton sees only the surfaces you wire up: nothing else in the customer's stack, nothing else in yours.
